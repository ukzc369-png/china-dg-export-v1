# How to Export Dichloromethane from China

Dichloromethane, also known as methylene chloride or DCM, is a widely used industrial solvent in pharmaceutical processing, metal cleaning, adhesives, coatings, foam production and chemical synthesis. Although it has legitimate industrial applications, its toxicity, volatility and regulatory sensitivity mean that an export shipment must be planned carefully.

This guide explains the main issues buyers should confirm before sourcing and shipping dichloromethane from China. Requirements can vary by product specification, packing method, port, carrier and destination country, so every shipment should be reviewed individually before booking.

## Product and Transport Classification

Common reference information for dichloromethane includes:

- Chemical name: Dichloromethane / Methylene Chloride
- Abbreviation: DCM
- CAS number: 75-09-2
- UN number: UN 1593
- Transport class: Class 6.1, toxic substance
- Packing group: III

Dichloromethane is generally not handled as a flammable liquid for transport, but it is volatile and harmful when inhaled. The current supplier SDS and the applicable transport rules must always be checked before packing, declaration and booking.

## Is Dichloromethane a Precursor Chemical in China?

This point requires careful wording. Dichloromethane is not listed in the general three-category catalogue attached to China’s Regulations on the Administration of Precursor Chemicals. It should not be confused with chloroform, or trichloromethane, which is listed separately.

However, dichloromethane may be subject to additional controls when exported to specified countries or regions under China’s destination-specific rules for chemicals that may be used in illicit drug manufacture. Import-country controls may also apply. Exporters and buyers should therefore confirm the destination, end user, declared use and current licensing requirements before accepting an order.

## Documents Commonly Required

A compliant shipment normally begins with a consistent document package. Depending on the transaction and destination, this may include:

- Commercial invoice and packing list
- Sales contract or purchase order
- Current safety data sheet (SDS)
- Certificate of analysis (COA)
- Dangerous goods classification or transport condition report, when required
- Customs declaration information
- Dangerous goods declaration and carrier documents
- Certificate of origin, if requested
- End-user and end-use statement for sensitive destinations or customers
- Import permit or other destination-country approval, where applicable

The product name, CAS number, UN number, specification, quantity and packing information should be consistent across all documents. Even small inconsistencies can delay booking, customs review or destination clearance.

## Packing Options

Dichloromethane is commonly supplied in suitable steel drums, ISO tanks or other approved packaging selected according to quantity and logistics conditions. The exact packing option must be confirmed against the SDS, packaging performance requirements, carrier acceptance rules and destination regulations.

Before loading, the shipper should verify:

- Packaging compatibility with dichloromethane
- UN packaging marks and validity, when required
- Closure condition and leak prevention
- Net and gross weight limits
- Correct Class 6.1 labels and marks
- UN 1593 shipping name and identification
- Container securing and segregation requirements

Because dichloromethane is volatile, packaging integrity and temperature exposure during storage and transport deserve particular attention.

## Export Booking and Port Execution

Dangerous goods bookings normally require more preparation time than general cargo. The exporter should confirm carrier acceptance, sailing schedule, documentation cut-off and port declaration procedures before arranging production and delivery to the terminal.

A practical workflow is:

1. Confirm the buyer, destination, end use and required specification.
2. Review destination-specific export and import controls.
3. Match the SDS, COA and transport classification with the sales documents.
4. Select compliant packaging and calculate the loading plan.
5. Submit the dangerous goods booking to an experienced carrier or forwarder.
6. Complete customs and dangerous goods declarations.
7. Deliver the container within the port cut-off and monitor loading.
8. Send the final shipping documents to the buyer for clearance.

Shipping arrangements should not be finalized until the carrier has accepted the cargo information and the buyer has confirmed its import capability.

## Buyer Due Diligence and End-Use Review

For regulatory-sensitive chemicals, price and availability are not the only considerations. A responsible exporter may request the buyer’s company registration, business scope, delivery address, end-use description and import authorization.

Buyers should be prepared to provide accurate information about:

- The importing company and final consignee
- Intended industrial application
- Expected purchase quantity and frequency
- Storage and handling capability
- Required local permits
- Whether the goods will be resold or transferred

Unclear end use, inconsistent company information, unusual routing or refusal to provide compliance documents should be investigated before shipment.

## Common Causes of Delay

Typical problems include using an outdated SDS, entering inconsistent cargo details, selecting unsuitable packaging, missing a carrier’s dangerous goods cut-off, or discovering destination licensing requirements after the vessel has been booked.

These risks can be reduced by completing a product-and-destination compliance review before issuing the final quotation. The quotation should clearly state the assumed specification, packing, Incoterm, loading quantity, port and document scope.
